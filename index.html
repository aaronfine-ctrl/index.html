The Space Between Who I Am
and Who Others See

“This section documents a semester-long process of collecting, comparing, and honestly interrogating two distinct pictures of myself: the one I carry internally, and the one I project outward. The data did not always agree. Understanding why — and what to do about it — is the most professionally useful work I have done this year.”

The data I collected

Five Sources, One Coherent Picture

Over the course of this semester, I gathered identity and reputation data through five structured instruments: a Self-Perception Inventory (a behavioral communication assessment measuring proactivity, reactivity, and work environment preferences), a Professional Reputation Survey (peer and professional feedback on adjectives, warmth, and effectiveness), a 360° Team Evaluation (teammate ratings on communication, reliability, and collaboration), a Core Values Exploration (a values-ranking exercise connecting personal beliefs to professional behavior), and a Growth Mindset Assessment (instructor and peer observations on how I respond to feedback and challenge).

Taken individually, each instrument offers a snapshot. Taken together, they form a pattern — and patterns are where the real insight lives.

Identity — how I see myself

I See Myself as an Operator

My Self-Perception Inventory placed me squarely in the Driver communication style: task-oriented, direct, efficiency-seeking, and in control of pace. Drivers initiate rather than wait, solve rather than escalate, and measure success by output. My Composite Assertiveness/Proactivity score of +1.0 confirmed this — I am more proactive than the average respondent, consistently initiating action before being asked.

My Composite Emotiveness/Reactivity score of −5.0 is the most extreme data point in my entire profile. It means I am highly non-reactive: under pressure, conflict, or uncertainty, I do not become emotional — I become analytical. This is deeply consistent with my stated core values of accountability and reliability. You cannot be reliably accountable if your emotional state shifts with the difficulty of the situation.

My work environment preferences reinforced this picture. I strongly prefer flat hierarchies (score: 1.00 — low preference for formal authority structures), which reflects a belief that good ideas and decisive action should outweigh rank. My feedback directness score of 0.33 confirmed I am comfortable giving and receiving blunt, specific criticism — something most people actively avoid.

Taken together, my self-image is someone who runs on structure, accountability, and forward motion. I see myself as the person who builds the plan and executes it.

Reputation — how others see me

Others See a Different Person Entirely

The adjectives my Professional Reputation Survey respondents selected to describe me were: hard-working, driven, respectable, social, approachable, and a strong communicator. Notice what is absent: structured, efficient, task-focused, analytical. The words I use to describe myself did not appear in how others describe me.

My warmth score of 5.45 out of 6 placed me in the Charismatic Zone — a research framework that maps perceived effectiveness against perceived warmth. The Charismatic Zone is the upper-right quadrant: high warmth, high effectiveness. It is also the rarest quadrant, because most professionals who project strong effectiveness do so at the expense of warmth — appearing competent but cold. My data suggests I have avoided that tradeoff, though not necessarily by design.

My 360° team evaluation deepened this picture. Teammates consistently described me using relational language: trusted, easy to work with, makes the group feel cohesive. The constructive feedback was pointed and specific: trust your teammates more, delegate ownership rather than just assigning tasks, and don’t let a relaxed mode read as disengagement. These are not criticisms of my values — they are observations about behaviors that contradict the warmth I project when I am operating in my Driver mode.

Cross-data patterns

What the Data Keeps Saying

When I look across all five instruments, three patterns emerge consistently enough to treat as reliable findings rather than noise:

✨
Pattern 1: Core values are confirmed across both self-perception and reputation data
The Core Values Exploration ranked accountability first and reliability second in my self-perception. In the Professional Reputation Survey, peers independently selected “hard-working,” “driven,” and “respectable” — adjectives that directly reflect those same values from an external vantage point. When the same traits surface in both self-reported and peer-reported data, the finding is reliable: my values are not only internal commitments — they are behaviorally visible to the people I work with.
◆
Pattern 2: Low reactivity scores do not appear in reputation adjectives
My Composite Emotiveness/Reactivity score of −5.0 is the most extreme data point in my Self-Perception Inventory, indicating very low emotional reactivity under pressure. However, reputation survey respondents did not select adjectives like “calm,” “steady,” or “composed.” The data suggests this trait, while present, is not consistently visible to others. One explanation supported by the 360° feedback: in non-leadership contexts, low reactivity can present as a neutral or passive mode rather than an active strength, making it harder for observers to register and name.
△
Pattern 3: Warmth scores are high and consistent across multiple instruments
My warmth score of 5.45/6 in the Professional Reputation Survey aligns with a Work Environment Preference score of 3.0/3 for relationship-building — the maximum possible. Two independent instruments, one self-reported and one peer-reported, both indicate a strong relational orientation. This convergence is meaningful: it means warmth is not a perception artifact, but a genuine behavioral pattern that others observe consistently across different contexts. The data places me in the upper-right Charismatic Zone, where high warmth and strong effectiveness intersect.
■
Pattern 4: Response to feedback is specific rather than general
Growth mindset data from instructor and peer observations indicates that I respond most effectively to specific, outcome-oriented feedback rather than general or process-based guidance. This is consistent with the Driver communication style profile from the Self-Perception Inventory, which identifies a preference for clear goals and measurable targets. The data suggests that framing development objectives in concrete, behavioral terms — rather than abstract qualities — produces the most observable progress.
Tensions and contradictions

The Contradictions Worth Sitting With

Clean data would tell a simple story. My data is not entirely clean. Three genuine tensions exist between my identity and my reputation that I cannot resolve by simply adjusting behavior — they require understanding.

Tension 1 — Control vs. trust

I value flat hierarchies (score: 1.00) and believe strongly that teams function best when authority is earned, not assumed. And yet my most consistent peer feedback was that I hold on too tight — I delegate tasks but not ownership, which means I am functionally recreating a hierarchy with myself at the top. I believe in flat structures in principle but practice something closer to benevolent control in execution. This is not hypocrisy; it is a gap between my values and my instincts. My Driver style wants to ensure outcomes. My values want to trust people. Those two impulses have not yet been reconciled.

Tension 2 — Confidence felt vs. confidence projected

I experience genuine internal confidence in structured environments where I know the domain and have a clear role. My perceived confidence score of ~0.5 suggests others do not consistently receive that signal. The gap is likely situational: my confidence is visible when I am leading or presenting, but diminishes when I am in a passive or unfamiliar role. My confidence is not absent — it is conditional, and conditions aren’t always visible to observers. In government finance settings, where credibility is often established in passive settings like budget reviews or interagency meetings, this is a meaningful gap to close.

Tension 3 — The presentation gap

My public speaking growth this semester revealed a specific contradiction: my communication content was consistently strong — structured, clear, and well-reasoned — but my delivery undercut it. By relying on memorization rather than internalization, I sounded polished at the cost of sounding present. This matters because a Driver’s instinct is to over-prepare in order to control outcomes — but in communication, over-preparation produces exactly the disconnection that undermines credibility. The fix was not to prepare less. It was to prepare differently: frameworks instead of scripts, presence instead of performance.

Longitudinal growth

Where I Started, Where I Landed, What Changed

Growth data is most credible when it tracks a specific, observable behavior over time — not a general sense of improvement. The clearest longitudinal arc in my data this semester is in public communication.

Start of semester — the control problem
Memorization as risk management
My instinct was to memorize speeches verbatim. I understood this as thorough preparation — eliminating uncertainty by removing improvisation. What I did not understand was that my audience could hear the difference between delivered content and lived content. Feedback from both instructor and peers used the word “rehearsed” — a word that sounds like a compliment but, in context, meant “disconnected from the room.” My Driver reflex to control the outcome was producing the opposite: less credibility, not more.
Mid-semester — the reframe
Frameworks replace scripts
The shift that worked was structural, not motivational. Instead of asking “how do I sound more natural?” I asked “how do I prepare differently?” The answer: build a mental architecture for each section — know the argument cold, leave the language open. Add intentional physical anchors: eye contact held for a full thought before moving, body oriented toward the audience rather than the slides, pauses used deliberately rather than avoided. These are Driver-compatible interventions: concrete, measurable, and improvable with practice.
End of semester — the gap that remains
Confident in small rooms, still developing at scale
By semester’s end, feedback had shifted. “Rehearsed” disappeared; “confident and engaging” appeared. But the improvement was clearest in small and mid-size presentations where I knew most of the room. In large, formal, or high-stakes settings — the kind that define senior government finance careers — my confidence at scale remains underdeveloped. I know what the gap is. I know why it exists. I do not yet have enough reps to have closed it.
Professional development going forward

What This Data Tells Me to Do Next

The purpose of this analysis is not self-knowledge for its own sake. It is to make better decisions about where to invest my professional development energy. Based on the patterns, tensions, and gaps documented above, three priorities are clear — each grounded directly in a specific data finding, not a general aspiration.

01
Restructure how I delegate
Source: 360° evaluation + Tension 1 (control vs. trust)
The feedback was specific: I delegate tasks, not ownership. The fix is behavioral and immediate. In every group project going forward, I will identify one decision domain I will not touch — and communicate that explicitly to my team. This is not about trusting less; it is about building the muscle of trusting more. In DoD finance environments, where cross-functional budget decisions require genuine deference to colleagues with different expertise, this is not optional growth — it is a prerequisite for advancement.
02
Make my engagement visible in passive modes
Source: Reputation gap + Pattern 2 (low reactivity is invisible)
My low-reactivity score (−5.0) is a genuine professional asset. But it produces a behavioral signature — stillness, economy of expression, minimal visible response — that others read as detachment rather than composure. The intervention is specific: in meetings and review sessions where I am listening rather than leading, I will add deliberate verbal tracking (“that connects to what you said earlier about X”) and physical signaling (forward lean, eye contact, note-taking visible to the room). I am not performing engagement. I am making real engagement legible.
03
Build a strategic confidence portfolio
Source: Confidence gap (~0.5) + Tension 2 + growth timeline
My perceived confidence score (~0.5) lags significantly behind my warmth score (5.45). Closing that gap requires deliberate exposure to the specific contexts where my confidence falters: large formal presentations, unfamiliar rooms, and settings where I am not the acknowledged expert. Over the next year, I will seek at least three opportunities to present to groups of 50 or more — whether through DCTC engagements, academic conferences, or professional events — and treat each as a training rep, not a performance. My warmth is already established. Confidence at scale is the final piece of the Charismatic Zone profile.
The synthesis

My professional persona is not the person I thought I was — it is more interesting than that.

I entered this process expecting the data to confirm my self-image: a disciplined, task-focused operator with strong accountability instincts. What I found was that confirmation plus something I had not named. Others do not primarily experience my discipline. They experience my warmth. And the combination — structure on the inside, charisma on the outside — turns out to be relatively rare in finance, and genuinely valuable in the government and DoD environments I am targeting.

The work going forward is not to become a different professional. It is to close the gaps between what I intend, what I project, and what others receive — and to do it in the specific, behavioral, Driver-compatible ways this data points toward. I do not want to manage impressions. I want my reputation to be an accurate reflection of my identity. This semester showed me how far that project has come — and exactly where it still has to go.
